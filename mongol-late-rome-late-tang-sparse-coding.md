# Cross-Coding: Mongol Fragmentation Under the Literary-Sparse Rule

**Date:** 2026-05-08
**Authoring tool:** DeepSeek
**Streams:** Three
**Purpose:** Apply the v3 literary-sparse asymmetry rule to the Mongol Empire fragmentation case (mongol-empire-fragmentation), cross-coding against Late Rome and Late Tang where literary corpora are abundant.

---

## 1. THE LITERARY-SPARSE ASYMMETRY RULE (v3)

As codified in methodology v3: *In cases where the historical literary corpus falls below the minimum threshold (fewer than three contemporaneous, authored texts directly addressing system conditions), the literary-strategic module cannot be applied at full resolution. The case may still be coded on quantitative and institutional variables, but the absence of literary signal must be explicitly noted. The absence is not itself a collapse signal; it is a data limitation. Do not impute collapse consciousness from silence.*

The rule is mandatory for cases below the threshold.

---

## 2. MONGOL FRAGMENTATION CASE: LITERARY CORPUS ASSESSMENT

| Criterion | Status | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Contemporaneous authored texts addressing system conditions | **Below threshold.** The *Secret History of the Mongols* (c. 1228–1240) is the closest text, but it is a dynastic origin narrative, not a collapse-registering work. | Written by and for the Mongol elite; it celebrates founding, not failure. |
| Direct witness texts comparable to Du Fu or Augustine | **None identified.** No Mongol author produced a work like *City of God* or "Spring Prospect" during the fragmentation period. | Mongol literary culture was primarily oral (epics, genealogical recitations) and administrative (the *Altan Debter*, the *jarlig* records). |
| External literary sources (Persian, Chinese) | Persian historians (Rashid al-Din, Juvayni) documented the Mongol collapse from an outsider perspective, but these are not the system's own literary corpus. | Useful as historical sources but do not fulfill the literary-strategic module's requirement for *internal* self-understanding. |

**Decision: The Mongol fragmentation case falls below the literary-strategic module threshold.** The full literary-strategic coding (legitimacy narrative, golden-age nostalgia, elite self-indictment, etc.) cannot be applied. The case will be coded on quantitative and institutional variables only, with the literary-sparse flag noted.

---

## 4. METHODOLOGICAL IMPLICATION

The literary-sparse rule prevents a false equivalence: it would be a methodological error to code the Mongol case's literary-strategic variables as "absent" and treat that absence as a collapse signal. The Mongols did not produce collapse literature because their literary culture was predominantly oral and administrative, not because they were or were not experiencing collapse. The absence is a data limitation, not a finding.

For the project's cross-case analysis, the Mongol case will be compared to Rome and Tang on the variables where data exists (temporal coding, quantifiable variables, system-type classification). The literary-strategic signals will not be compared cross-case with the Mongol case, to avoid imputing meaning to silence.
