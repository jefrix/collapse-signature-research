Typology and Schema Fit
Our three Stream One cases do indeed cluster into a typology: Oracle was a vehicle-mediated buyer (it financed AI contracts through customer prepayments and third-party lessors), Microsoft an equity-investor/platform partner (it holds a stake in OpenAI and provides Azure infrastructure), and Amazon a pure-platform provider (AWS hosting AI models with no equity stake or special financing). Under the v3 schema, these roles remain distinct, but the discriminating power of some fields has changed. For example, actor_legal_role_split cleanly separates Oracle from Microsoft and Amazon (buyer vs investor/provider), and the customer_funding_mode and obligation_recognition_state fields only fired for Oracle, as intended. However, this also means those fields have limited cross-case utility. In that sense, the schema might appear over-fitted: Oracle’s “customer-supplied GPU” mode is very case-specific and never repeats, and the guarantee_support field (the canal-shipment analog) was used only for Oracle’s leasing deal. If future cases lack such unique mechanics, those fields may remain idle. In practice, the typology stands up but at the cost of having some one-off columns. This suggests we should remain cautious: fields triggered only in one case might reflect novel facts rather than generalizable signatures.

Another potential overfit is our treatment of counterparty concentration. Microsoft and Amazon were coded as moderate/low concentration simply because their companies are very large. That worked for these examples, but it blurs the economic logic: a single giant stake (Microsoft) was similar to shared service usage (Amazon) in our code. The schema could be asked to distinguish, say, a $50B stake in a startup versus a $50B cloud-services contract, even though both are "big". Our framework mostly grouped them by portfolio share. This may be a weaker discrimination than we first assumed.

Chokepoint Dependency
In the historical briefs, chokepoint dependency repeatedly emerged: Western Rome’s reliance on North African grain; Tang’s reliance on one major canal; Eastern Rome’s reliance on Egyptian grain. We sought analogous bottlenecks in our cases. Oracle’s “grain-shipment paradox” is the closest real analog: it depended on the Changjiang canal for rice after losing northern farms. In pure schema terms, this was captured by the compensatory-fragility logic. In Microsoft’s case, I cannot find a single chokepoint; Azure is global and Google Tensor chips (for example) can come from multiple sources. One possible chokepoint is the limited supply of advanced GPUs or AI chips (a current industry constraint), but we did not formalize that. Amazon similarly has a diversified datacenter network and no obvious single failure point. If forced to name something, it might be AWS’s need for specialized ARM-based processors (Graviton chips) or power capacity, but these are diffuse and managed at scale. In short, no clear single chokepoint stands out in Microsoft or Amazon. This gap might reflect that modern cloud platforms are designed to avoid single points of failure, unlike pre-industrial societies. The lack of an obvious chokepoint suggests that variable may be less directly applicable in these cases, or that our model lacks a formal credit for supply-chain concentration.

Next-Case Tradeoff
Looking ahead, the choice of the next case hinges on testing known patterns versus exploring blind spots. A company like CoreWeave (or Crusoe) would test our existing hypothesis: it is a pure GPU/cloud operator that explicitly raises private credit, much like Oracle’s model on a smaller scale. If CoreWeave’s case follows the same “vehicle + private debt” pattern, that reinforces the Oracle insight. In contrast, Google (Alphabet) or Meta would probe a blind spot. Google is a cloud/platform provider with internal R&D (language models, etc.) but minimal outside financing, and Meta is a social-platform giant also pushing AI internally. These cases differ from all three we have: they have deep pockets and built-in AI corpora, with no external lessors or equity stakes. They might reveal new channels (for example, how a fully in-house R&D and datacenter expansion is financed, or how narrative plays out in a social context).

Thus, the tradeoff is clear: CoreWeave/Crusoe tests our current hypothesis (private-finance-enabled AI firms) by looking for confirmation or nuance in a similar setup. Google or Meta test a potential blind spot (what happens when the system-type is “modern liberal-bureaucratic tech behemoth” with internal R&D, beyond our current triad). A good answer might involve examining one of each: for example, CoreWeave to strengthen or refine the private-credit pattern, and Google/Meta to expand the typology beyond our existing spectrum. The decision depends on whether we value reinforcing a pattern we’ve already identified (hypothesis-testing) or exploring the edges of our framework (blind-spot exploration).
