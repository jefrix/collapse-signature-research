# Cross-Coding: Late Rome (ROME-395) vs. Late Tang (TANG-755)

**Date:** 2026-05-08
**Authoring tool:** DeepSeek
**Streams:** Three (civilizational-literary), with Stream One/Two implications
**Taxonomy:** v3 system-type coding (conquest empire vs. agrarian-bureaucratic empire)
**Purpose:** Formal comparative table testing the collapse-signature framework across two major case studies from different civilizational traditions.

---

## 1. SYSTEM-TYPE COMPARISON

| Variable | Late Tang (TANG-755) | Late Rome West (ROME-395) |
|---|---|---|
| System type (v3) | Agrarian-bureaucratic empire | Conquest empire (tributary) |
| Foundational logic | Administrative extraction from a stable territorial base via land tax and household registration | Extraction from conquered provinces; expansion as revenue source |
| Core revenue base | Yangtze delta (rice), salt monopoly, land tax | Africa Proconsularis (grain), Gaul (taxes), Spain |
| Collapse pathway | Internal rebellion → fiscal collapse → simplification-survival within dynasty | Frontier breach → loss of tribute provinces → fiscal-military downward spiral → successor kingdoms |
| Elite recruitment | Examination system (partially disrupted but maintained) | Senatorial aristocracy (tax-evading), military-bureaucratic elite |
| Military-fiscal structure | Standing army funded by land tax and monopolies | Field army funded by provincial taxation; reliance on *foederati* |
| Geographic resilience | Core provinces retained (Yangtze, southeast); capital Chang'an exposed but recaptured | Core (Italy) exposed; grain chokepoint (Africa) lost; capital Rome sacked |

## 3. COMPARATIVE LITERARY-STRATEGIC CODING

| Variable | Late Tang | Late Rome West | Convergence/Divergence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Legitimacy narrative | **Collapsed.** The Confucian imperial bargain is documented as broken. | **De-coupled.** Augustine separates the City of God from the earthly city. | Both experience legitimacy collapse, but Tang legitimacy remains attached to the dynasty; Roman legitimacy detaches from the state entirely. **Divergent response** to similar signal. |
| Return-to-foundations | **Present but marginal** (Han Yu). | **Dominant and transformative** (Augustine). | The strength of this variable predicts the severity of institutional dissolution: stronger return-to-foundations associated with complete state replacement. |

| Elite self-indictment | **Present but gentled** (Bai Juyi elegizes the emperor; Du Fu documents suffering). | **Strong and explicit** (Salvian: "We are ourselves the barbarians."). | The intensity of elite self-indictment is higher in the Roman case, where collapse was total. |
| Apocalyptic/providential | **Absent.** Collapse is historical, not cosmic. | **Present but modulated.** Augustine counters apocalypticism but the anxiety is widespread. | **Divergent.** Christian eschatology provides a specific vocabulary for collapse that Confucianism lacks. |

## 4. CAPACITY-THROUGHPUT-RESILIENCE COMPARISON

| Component | Late Tang | Late Rome West |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-collapse capacity | High: household registration, granary system, Grand Canal, standing army | High: Diocletianic tax system, *annona* grain fleet, road network, field army |
| Pre-collapse throughput | High: Grand Canal grain shipments, salt monopoly distribution | High: African grain to Rome, Egyptian grain to Constantinople |
| Resilience (vulnerability to chokepoint loss) | **Medium-low.** The salt monopoly was a narrow chokepoint but survived because the producing regions (Huai River) were held. The canal was periodically cut. | **Catastrophically low.** The African annona was a single chokepoint. When Carthage fell, Rome's grain supply collapsed. |
| Post-collapse adaptation | Simplified but survived: salt monopoly provided narrow but reliable revenue. | Fragmentation: post-Roman kingdoms operated at much lower complexity. |
| Compensatory-fragility signature | **Present.** High grain throughput masked capital dependency; when the canal was cut (783), famine struck. | **Present and fatal.** High grain throughput masked total dependency on Africa; loss of Carthage (439) meant irreversible urban collapse. |

---

## 5. KEY FINDINGS FOR THE COLLAPSE SIGNATURE

1. **Rising-vs-declining self-narrative converges at -2 for both collapses.** This confirms the variable is consistently present in collapse cases (though causality direction remains under investigation).

2. **Return-to-foundations intensity correlates with collapse severity.** Where return-to-foundations is dominant (Late Rome) → successor kingdoms replaced the state. Where it is marginal (Late Tang) → dynasty survived in simplified form.

3. **Chokepoint dependency is a structural vulnerability marker.** Both cases show high-capacity, high-throughput systems that were critically vulnerable to single-chokepoint loss. This may be a general collapse signature.

4. **System-type taxonomy provides explanatory power.** The conquest empire (Rome) collapsed into fragments; the agrarian-bureaucratic empire (Tang) simplified but persisted. The system type shapes the *form* of collapse.
