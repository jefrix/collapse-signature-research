## Screening Step 1 — Corpus Availability Check

**Elite discourse corpus:** The Holy Roman Empire after 1648 possessed a rich public law discourse (*Reichspublizistik*). Treatises by Samuel von Pufendorf, Johann Jakob Moser, and others explicitly discussed the empire’s constitutional defects. Pufendorf’s famous description of the empire as a “monstro simile” (1667) is an internal elite critique. Enlightenment journals and pamphlets further documented perceived imperial weakness.  
**Adequacy for self‑indictment coding:** Adequate.  
**Decision:** Proceed with full variable coding.

---

## Screening Step 2 — MNBE Classification Test

**Criteria from MNBE memo:**

1. *Constitutional recognition of nationalities?*  
   The Imperial Estates (Reichsstände) represented territorial rulers, not nationalities per se. The empire did not institutionalize national communities in a way comparable to the Habsburg or Soviet models. Score: **does not meet criterion**.

2. *Supranational legitimacy claim non‑coterminous with any single ethnicity?*  
   Yes. The empire claimed to be the temporal arm of Christendom, the *Sacrum Romanum Imperium*. This universalist claim transcended German ethnicity, though in practice it was closely tied to German‑speaking lands. Partially meets criterion.

3. *Nationality‑based defection as primary collapse mechanism?*  
   No. The empire’s dissolution in 1806 resulted from internal consolidation of territorial sovereignty and Napoleonic pressure, not nationalist secessions. The Confederation of the Rhine was a realignment of princely states, not a national liberation movement. Score: **does not meet criterion**.

**Classification result:** The HRE is **not** a Multi‑National Bureaucratic Empire. It is a pre‑modern feudal‑corporatist imperial structure that evolved into a loose confederation of sovereign principalities. This partial classification is valuable because it allows us to compare the MNBE terminal-speed dynamic with a non‑MNBE, non‑bureaucratic multi‑national entity.

---

## 3. Temporal Coding

**Onset window:** 1648 (Peace of Westphalia) to 1806. Westphalia formally recognized imperial estates’ sovereignty, locking in the simplification.  
**Terminal trigger:** 12 July 1806 (Treaty of the Confederation of the Rhine; Francis II abdicates 6 August 1806).  
**Terminal speed:** The simplification was gradual, spanning 158 years. No single rapid collapse event. For the purpose of the MNBE prediction, we measure from the acceleration point: French Revolutionary Wars (1792) and Lunéville (1801), leading to the Reichsdeputationshauptschluss (1803) and dissolution in 1806 — a terminal phase of ~14 years, but this is an analytical selection.

**Trajectory interpretation:** NRC was chronically low‑medium, with gradual erosion. Terminal NRC hit 0.0 only in 1806. The empire survived for centuries at low NRC because no sudden shock shattered it; it was gradually hollowed out.

---

## 5. Outcome Classification

Not a collapse in the Tainter sense of rapid simplification. The HRE is a case of **protracted simplification** (1648–1806) with a terminal dissolution phase (1801–1806). This does not fit the collapse signature model well.

---

## 6. MNBE Terminal-Speed Prediction Assessment

The MNBE model predicts that low NRC without retraction leads to fast collapse (e.g., Soviet 6.8 y, Habsburg 4.3 y). The HRE had low NRC but slow terminal pace. The discrepancy reinforces that MNBE type requires *bureaucratic* administration and *nationality‑based defection* to produce the rapid collapse dynamic. The HRE’s failure on those criteria explains why it does not replicate the MNBE speed pattern. This is analytically valuable.
