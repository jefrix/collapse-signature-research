## 1. DATING METHODOLOGY  

The Ming consolidation is the recovery leg of a two-part sequence: Yuan collapse (1351–1368) followed by Ming re-complexification (1368–1424). The collapse of the Yuan is genuine under Tainter’s definition—rapid simplification across multiple domains. The Ming then rebuilt bureaucratic complexity from the collapse floor. This makes the case a recovery case (not a transformation within an existing regime, and not a near-collapse that prevented simplification). It therefore sits in the control group as an example of successful recovery after collapse.

| Field | Value | Confidence | Evidence basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Baseline period | 1260–1340 CE (Yuan at administrative peak, especially Shizu and early Renzong) | B | Yuan fiscal and administrative records; *Yuan Shi*; Marco Polo descriptions (late 13th c.) |
| Stress-accumulation period | c. 1340–1351 CE | B | Yellow River floods 1344, 1349; inflationary paper-currency crisis; factional conflicts at court; banditry and famine reports in *Yuan Shi* annals |

**Outcome classification for the Yuan:** COLLAPSE. The Yuan state in China proper lost fiscal extraction, territorial control, and the bureaucratic-administrative apparatus. The remaining rump state in Mongolia was a steppe polity, not a successor bureaucratic empire. The collapse meets the Tainter criteria: rapid simplification sustained with no restoration.

**Classification of Ming consolidation:** RECOVERY. The Ming state re-established a complex bureaucratic empire with comparable or greater territorial reach, population registration, fiscal capacity, and military organization than the Yuan had possessed in China proper. The recovery fits the project’s definition: restoration of complexity to at least the previous baseline within one generation.

| Criterion | Assessment |

| Does the case cleanly fit the “recovery” slot, or is it more complicated? | It fits the recovery slot cleanly for the dataset’s purposes, with two caveats: (a) the Yuan collapse was partly a collapse of Mongol imperial overreach, not just internal complexity crisis; (b) the Ming never recovered the full Yuan territorial extent (Mongolia, Tibet, Manchuria in part), so if the system boundary is the entire Mongol empire, it’s a transformation to a smaller-scale Chinese empire rather than full recovery. **Honest note on complication:** If we define the pre-collapse system as the Mongol world-empire (Yuan including Mongolia, Manchuria, Tibet, and the steppe dependencies), then the Ming recovery was incomplete—the system reorganized at smaller scale. However, for comparability with the Late Tang (which also contracted territorially), we bound the system as the Chinese dynastic state. The loss of non-Han territories is a recurrent feature of Chinese dynastic collapse-recovery cycles and is treated as a scaling-down rather than a classification-breaking complication.

## 4. LITERARY-STRATEGIC MODULE  

### 4.1 Genre Shift from Late Yuan to Early Ming  

This is a structural observation worth coding. The late Yuan literary corpus is dominated by:

- **Poetry of lament and retreat:** Ni Zan, Wu Zhen, Yang Weizhen—literati who withdrew from the collapsing Yuan court and wrote of reclusion, loss, and the end of an era.
- **Rebel millenarian language:** The Red Turbans used Buddhist-Manichaean rhetoric (“Maitreya has come,” cleansing the world of evil).
- **Chronicles of chaos:** Local gazetteers and private histories that record banditry, famine, and administrative breakdown.

The early Ming literary corpus, by contrast, shifts dramatically to:

- **Dynastic legitimization:** Zhu Yuanzhang’s own edicts, the *Ming Taizu Shilu*, official histories.
- **Imperial encyclopedism:** The Yongle Encyclopedia (22,877 chapters) as a claim to comprehensive civilizational order.
- **Court-commissioned poetry and prose:** Songs of dynastic founding, examination essays in the new Neo-Confucian orthodoxy.

The genre shift itself—from lament/withdrawal to legitimization/order—may be a signature of the transition from collapse to recovery. In collapse cases, literature documents suffering; in recovery cases, literature constructs legitimacy. This pattern is worth tracking across the full dataset.

## 5. RISING-VERSUS-DECLINING NARRATIVE CLASSIFICATION  

**Coding: UNAMBIGUOUSLY RISING (+2)**  

The Ming founding literature frames the new dynasty as the restoration of legitimate Chinese rule after a century of Mongol domination. The key term is *恢復* (to restore, to recover). The narrative is:

- The Yuan was a period of alien misrule (illegitimate).
- Heaven has withdrawn the mandate from the Yuan and granted it to a Han Chinese commoner who has proven his worth.
- The new dynasty will restore the institutions, borders, and cultural confidence of the Han-Tang-Song golden age.  

This is a forward-looking narrative even when it references the past, because the past is invoked as a model to be *rebuilt*, not as a lost world to be mourned. The temporal orientation is toward the future: the Hongwu and Yongle reigns are constructing a new order, not managing decline.

**Contrast with Late Tang:** The Tang post-755 corpus is saturated with backward-looking elegy; the past is a site of loss. The Ming corpus is saturated with restoration rhetoric; the past is a site of legitimation. The variable discriminates cleanly between the two cases.

**Confidence in classification: Level A.** The self-narrative is unambiguous in the founding texts.
