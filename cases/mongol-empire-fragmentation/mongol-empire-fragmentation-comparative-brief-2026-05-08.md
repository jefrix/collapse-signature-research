## 3. LITERARY-CULTURAL FOUNDATION AND ASYMMETRY  

### 3.1 Late-Period Corpora by Khanate  

**Yuan**  
Rich Chinese-language official histories (*Yuan Shi* compiled under Ming), poetry of retreat (Ni Zan, Yang Weizhen), and rebel millenarian literature. Decline-consciousness abundant.

**Ilkhanate**  
Persian historiography: Rashid al-Din’s *Jami‘ al-tawarikh* (completed 1310) and its continuators (Wassaf, Hamdallah Mustawfi). Post-Abu Sa‘id chronicles (Hafiz-i Abru, 15th c.) describe fragmentation. Persian poetry of lament (Ibn Yamin) and didactic works. The Ilkhanate is the best-documented of the three western khanates for literary signals.

**Chagatai Khanate**  
Extremely sparse contemporary narrative. Literary sources are almost all later Timurid constructions (*Tarikh-i Rashidi*, *Zafarnama*). No substantial late-period corpus survives from the Chagatai khanate itself. The methodological challenge is acute.

**Golden Horde**  
No continuous chronicle from within the khanate. Rus’ chronicles (e.g., Nikon Chronicle) record events externally; Mamluk diplomatic correspondence provides some insight. Turkic oral epic traditions (Edige, Nogay) were later written down but contain nostalgic kernels. The absence of a self-generating literary corpus is itself a signal of low institutional complexity.

### 3.2 Asymmetry Rule (Proposed)  

The project should adopt a rule for cases with thin or absent late-period literary corpora:  
*When a case lacks a substantial contemporary literary corpus, (a) flag the case as “literary-sparse,” (b) rely on proxy literary signals from successor or neighboring polities, and (c) reduce the weight of literary-strategic variables in signature scoring, avoiding the inference that absence of lament means absence of decline. The literary-strategic module should be treated as optional for such cases.*

**Comparative note:** Where literary sources exist (Yuan, Ilkhanate), the self-narrative is declining. The Yuan’s decline narrative is partially the voice of the subject population rather than the Mongol court, complicating coding. But overall, the Mongol successor states produced no strong rising narrative from within the collapsing regime itself. This fits the pattern that collapse polities rarely narrate themselves as rising.

## 5. COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS: SHARED STRUCTURAL FEATURES OF MONGOL COLLAPSES  

### 5.1 Features Common to Mongol Khanates  

1. **Succession crises and lack of institutionalized rulership transfer.** All four khanates experienced rapid turnover of khans and violent succession struggles, rooted in the Mongol tradition of appanage and personal loyalty.
2. **Overextension and fiscal thinness.** The Mongol empire was built on continuous expansion; once expansion halted, revenue from plunder and tribute declined. The sedentary tax bases were often poorly integrated, and the regime relied on local administrators (Persian, Chinese, Rus’) who were not fully loyal.
3. **Ethnic division between ruling Mongols and subject populations.** Tensions persisted, and assimilation was limited except in the Yuan (where Mongol identity weakened anyway). This inhibited the formation of a unified elite with shared self-narrative.
4. **Military degradation.** The professional *tumen* structure gave way to powerful tribal lords (Qara’unas in Chagatai, Noyans in Horde) who fragmented military power.
5. **Environmental/disease shocks.** The Black Death (1330s–1340s) hit the Ilkhanate and Golden Horde particularly hard, compounding fiscal and demographic stress. Evidence: chronicles note massive mortality, abandonment of cities.

### 5.2 Comparison to Agrarian-Bureaucratic Collapses (Late Tang)  

| Feature | Late Tang | Mongol Khanates |
|---|---|---|
| Primary fiscal base | Broad land tax on registered households, then salt monopoly | Mixed: pastoral tribute, trade taxes, plunder; land tax often poorly administered |
| Succession mechanism | Hereditary imperial line with stable primogeniture | Tanistry-like competition; appanage system |
| Administrative depth | Deep, multi-tiered bureaucracy | Thin, dual Mongol/native layer |
| Collapse trigger | Military rebellion by frontier commander (An Lushan) | Multiple triggers: succession, plague, fiscal erosion |
| Outcome | Simplified state survives under same dynasty | Disintegration into minor states or replacement by new dynasties (Ming, Timurids) |

**Implication for the Yuan-to-Ming control case:** The Yuan collapse shares many features with other Mongol khanates—suggesting a distinct Mongol-imperial collapse signature. This complicates its use as a direct control for the Late Tang (a non-conquest agrarian-bureaucratic collapse). However, the Ming *recovery* phase is still a valid example of re-complexification after collapse, regardless of the collapse mechanism. The project should note that the Yuan collapse may not neatly match the same causal pathways as Tang, and therefore the recovery comparison must be weighted accordingly. The open question from the Ming brief (“Was Yuan collapse driven by Mongol imperial overreach rather than fiscal-demographic stress?”) can now be answered: It was both, but the Mongol-specific features were significant. This reduces the direct comparability of the control but does not invalidate it; instead, it enriches the dataset by providing a conquest-empire collapse type and its recovery.

## 7. OPEN QUESTIONS  

1. **Did the Black Death function as a common-mode shock that pushed multiple Mongol khanates over the threshold at roughly the same time?** If so, the timing coincidence suggests a systemic vulnerability independent of direct political linkage.  
2. **Why did the Ming recovery succeed in re-complexification while the post-Ilkhanid Iranian world remained fragmented until the Safavids?** This question bears on whether recovery requires a specific institutional legacy (Chinese bureaucracy vs. Persianate iqta‘ system).  
3. **Should the project treat “conquest-empire” as a distinct system type with its own collapse signature profile?** If yes, the Yuan case would be classified separately, and a different control might be needed for the Tang.  
4. **How to handle the asymmetry in literary corpus for non-literate or semi-literate polities?** The proposed rule (Section 3.2) needs to be formalized in the methodology.
