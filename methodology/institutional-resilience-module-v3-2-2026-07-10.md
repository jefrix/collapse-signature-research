# Institutional Resilience Module v3.2

**Date:** 2026-07-10  
**Status:** Draft for Claude methodology review  
**Scope:** Post-Bronze-Age states only. Not applied to Late Bronze Age cases.  
**Purpose:** Formalize the seven resilience variables required by the May 24 synthesis carry‑forward ruling. These variables assess a state’s capacity to withstand stress without structural simplification. They must be measured independently of self‑indictment register coding (see Section 8).

---

## 1. Center Coherence

**Operational definition:** The degree to which the central decision‑making body (monarch, executive, party leadership) can formulate and execute a unified policy without being paralyzed by factional conflict or constitutional stalemate.

**Scoring rubric (0–3):**
- 3 – High coherence: center speaks with one voice; factional disputes resolved within institutional bounds.
- 2 – Moderate: persistent factions but decisions still reachable; occasional deadlock.
- 1 – Low: center is chronically divided; major decisions delayed or contradictory.
- 0 – Absent: center has ceased to function as a single actor; parallel power centers exist.

**Anchor case:**  
- Late Qing (1898–1911): Score 1. Empress Dowager Cixi and reformist emperors/elites clashed; after 1908, the regency was divided and unable to hold provincial loyalty.

**Interaction with v3 variables:**  
- *NRC dynamic:* Low center coherence reduces the regime’s ability to control the public sphere (NRC Indicator 1).  
- *Self‑indictment register:* A divided center often produces self‑indictment from one faction against another, even if the regime as a whole does not formally self‑indict.

---

## 2. Fiscal Sovereignty

**Operational definition:** The state’s ability to raise revenue from its own territory and population without dependency on external actors that can attach policy conditions.

**Scoring rubric (0–3):**
- 3 – Full fiscal sovereignty: domestic tax base adequate; no external conditional lending.
- 2 – Largely sovereign, but some external borrowing under favorable terms.
- 1 – Partial loss: significant reliance on external creditors or tribute; conditionalities imposed.
- 0 – Fiscal sovereignty collapsed: external receivership or revenue controlled by foreign powers.

**Anchor case:**  
- Ottoman Empire (post‑1881): Score 1 after the Ottoman Public Debt Administration assumed control of key revenues. External stabilisation existed, but fiscal sovereignty was deeply compromised.

**Interaction with v3 variables:**  
- *NRC Indicator 3 (External Stabilisation):* Low fiscal sovereignty may provide external stabilisation that slows collapse even as it erodes domestic legitimacy.  
- *Administrative sovereignty:* Often correlates; loss of fiscal control usually implies loss of administrative autonomy.

---

## 3. Elite Integration

**Operational definition:** The extent to which the political, economic, and cultural elites see their interests tied to the survival of the existing regime and coordinate action toward that end.

**Scoring rubric (0–3):**
- 3 – High integration: elites actively support regime; defection rare.
- 2 – Moderate: elite cohesion exists but some sectors are ambivalent or partially exit.
- 1 – Low: significant elite groups have decoupled from the regime; internal opposition is open.
- 0 – Disintegration: elites have abandoned the regime and are constructing successor institutions.

**Anchor case:**  
- Habsburg (1914–1918): Score 1. German‑Austrian elites largely loyal, but Hungarian elites pursued separate interests, and Czech, Polish, and South Slav elites were actively building parallel national institutions.

**Interaction with v3 variables:**  
- *Self‑indictment register:* Elite self‑indictment usually reflects low elite integration, especially when internal (locus: internal).  
- *MNBE subtype:* Multi‑national empires often exhibit ethnically segmented elite integration; measuring it requires breaking down by national segment.

---

## 4. Command Continuity

**Operational definition:** The uninterrupted chain of authority from the center to the administrative and coercive apparatus, including military, police, and provincial administration.

**Scoring rubric (0–3):**
- 3 – Unbroken: central orders executed uniformly; no autonomous military or bureaucratic units.
- 2 – Minor disruptions: occasional provincial non‑compliance, but core apparatus intact.
- 1 – Significant erosion: military units or provinces refuse orders without consequences; warlords emerge.
- 0 – Collapse: center cannot command; armed forces fragment along regional or factional lines.

**Anchor case:**  
- Soviet Union (1989–1991): Score 1 declining to 0. Military largely held together until August 1991 coup’s failure; afterward, republics asserted control over local forces and central command evaporated.

**Interaction with v3 variables:**  
- *NRC dynamic:* Command continuity is a prerequisite for high regime control of the public sphere. Loss of command continuity makes retraction impossible.  
- *Administrative activity vs. sovereignty:* Activity (patrols, reports) may persist while command continuity erodes; the pair measures that gap.

---

## 5. Narrative‑Center Alignment

**Operational definition:** The degree to which the central regime’s public legitimizing narrative (rising, renewal, defensive, etc.) is shared by the broader elite and the administrative apparatus, rather than being a hollow official fiction.

**Scoring rubric (0–3):**
- 3 – Full alignment: narrative believed and actively propagated by elites and administrators.
- 2 – Broad acceptance with some cynical outliers.
- 1 – Widely disbelieved: narrative is maintained only by the center’s propaganda effort; elites comply outwardly but privately reject it.
- 0 – Narrative collapse: even the center no longer bothers to assert the narrative; public discourse dominated by counter‑narratives.

**Anchor case:**  
- Late Tang (post‑755): Score 1. The court maintained Confucian restoration rhetoric, but the literary elite overwhelmingly registered loss and decline; the narrative was hollow.

**Interaction with v3 variables:**  
- *Self‑indictment register:* Low narrative‑center alignment often precedes or coincides with the reformist register, and can transition to terminal register if the gap widens.  
- *NRC dynamic:* Retraction is easier when alignment is already low; retraction then acknowledges the pre‑existing reality.

---

## 6. Administrative Sovereignty

**Operational definition:** The state’s exclusive authority to make and enforce rules within its territory, free from effective veto by external actors or autonomous domestic power centers.

**Scoring rubric (0–3):**
- 3 – Full sovereignty: state law prevails; no external or internal veto.
- 2 – Mostly sovereign: minor exceptions (e.g., treaty obligations, local autonomy under central supervision).
- 1 – Compromised: foreign powers or regional strongmen exercise effective veto over key policy areas.
- 0 – Lost: state sovereignty is fictional; territory administered by outside entities or warlords.

**Anchor case:**  
- Late Qing (post‑1895): Score 1. Foreign concessions, treaty‑port extraterritoriality, and autonomous provincial governors rendered central administrative sovereignty severely compromised.

**Interaction with v3 variables:**  
- *Fiscal sovereignty:* Often moves in tandem; loss of administrative sovereignty usually undermines tax collection.  
- *Geographic defensibility binary:* High geographic defensibility may allow a state to preserve some administrative sovereignty even under external pressure.

---

## 7. Administrative Activity vs. Administrative Sovereignty

**Operational definition:** This pair captures the gap between the *appearance* of state activity (patrols, reports, decrees issued, bureaucratic transactions) and the state’s actual sovereign control. High activity with low sovereignty indicates a hollow state performing rituals of governance without effective power.

**Scoring rubric:**  
- Activity: qualitative (High / Medium / Low) based on bureaucratic output.  
- Sovereignty: measured by the Administrative Sovereignty score (0–3).  
- Gap = Activity level – Sovereignty score (converted to a 1–3 scale for comparison). Large gaps signal fragility.

**Anchor case:**  
- Ottoman Hamidian era: Activity high (extensive surveillance, reports, public works), Sovereignty 1 (foreign financial control, autonomous provinces). The gap was a signature of the rentier‑surveillance state.

**Interaction with v3 variables:**  
- *NRC dynamic:* The activity‑sovereignty gap is a measure of the regime’s capacity to project an image of control while losing actual control, directly relevant to NRC Indicator 1 (control of public sphere).  
- *Self‑indictment register:* Terminal self‑indictment often explicitly denounces the gap between official activity and real sovereignty.

---

## 8. Independence from Self‑Indictment Register Coding

The resilience variables described above must be scored using administrative, fiscal, and structural evidence independent of the self‑indictment register coding. The self‑indictment register (Reformist/Terminal) captures elite *perceptions* of resilience; the resilience variables capture the *structural* condition. A state may exhibit low center coherence and low fiscal sovereignty without its elite publicly articulating terminal self‑indictment (e.g., early Brezhnev USSR). Conversely, an elite may produce terminal self‑indictment while some structural resilience variables remain moderate (e.g., Habsburg 1914). Therefore, the two sets of variables must be measured on separate evidence and cross‑referenced in analysis, not conflated.
