# Failed‑Rising Hypothesis: Calibration Note

**Date:** 2026-05-13
**Authoring tool:** DeepSeek
**Purpose:** State the hypothesis with explicit success/failure criteria; identify generalising and falsifying cases; ensure the May 15 synthesis draft does not overclaim.

---

## 1. Statement of the hypothesis

> When a complex agrarian‑bureaucratic empire publicly commits itself to a narrative of renewal, rising, or restoration (the "rising narrative"), and that narrative subsequently fails in a visible, unambiguous event involving a central artifact of the promised renewal, the terminal collapse speed (time from failure event to loss of state sovereignty) is **significantly shorter** than in otherwise comparable cases where the pre‑failure narrative was already one of decline or where the narrative was deliberately retracted after failure.

**Operational components:**

- **Rising narrative:** Publicly articulated by the regime and its intellectual allies; promises institutional modernisation, territorial integrity, and restored greatness; finds expression in reform edicts, literature, journalism, and material symbols (fleets, railways, legal codes).
- **Visible failure event:** A military defeat, economic default, or institutional breakdown that directly contradicts the narrative's claims and is widely perceived as such by literate elites and the public.
- **Terminal speed:** Years from failure event to the effective loss of sovereignty (annexation, revolution that ends the dynasty, or foreign occupation).

---

## 2. Current evidence base

| Case | Narrative type | Failure event | Terminal speed (years) | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tang (post‑755) | Declining from start | (n/a – collapse already underway) | 144 y to terminal (907) | Simplification‑survival, slow |
| Qing (1895‑1911) | Rising (Self‑Strengthening) | Sino‑Japanese War / Beiyang Fleet | 16 y to revolution | Fast terminal collapse |
| Ottoman (1878‑1908‑1918) | Rising (Tanzimat) → retracted → re‑launched rising | 1877‑78 war; Balkan Wars/WWI | 41 y of non‑collapse after retraction; 10 y after re‑launch | Slow persistence under retraction; fast collapse after re‑launch |

The Tang‑Qing comparison demonstrates a speed differential consistent with the hypothesis.
The Ottoman case adds a **boundary condition**: deliberate retraction of the failed narrative can slow terminal speed, but if the narrative is re‑launched and fails again, speed returns to the fast trajectory.

Thus the hypothesis currently rests on **two successful tests** (Qing fast, Tang slow) and **one boundary‑condition refinement** (Ottoman retraction). The evidence is suggestive but not yet generalised.

---

## 3. Success criteria for the hypothesis

The hypothesis is considered **supported** if, across a dataset of at least five agrarian‑bureaucratic empires with clear narrative coding:

- Cases with a failed‑rising narrative (and no retraction) show a mean terminal speed of **≤ 25 years**.
- Cases with a declining narrative show a mean terminal speed of **≥ 80 years**.
- Cases with narrative retraction show intermediate speeds, and the retraction variable accounts for the deviation.

**Minimum confidence for a synthesis claim:** "The pattern is consistent with the hypothesis; two direct comparisons support it; further case testing is necessary before asserting the hypothesis as general."

## 4. Cases that would **generalise** the hypothesis

| Case | Narrative status | Expected outcome if FRH holds |
|---|---|---|
| Habsburg late‑empire (1867‑1918) | Modest rising narrative (Austro‑Hungarian Compromise), failure in WWI | Fast terminal collapse (2 y from 1916‑1918) – consistent |
| Soviet Union (1985‑1991) | Rising narrative (glasnost/perestroika), failure of reforms and loss of Eastern Europe | Fast terminal collapse (6 y) – consistent if narrative is coded as rising |
| Tokugawa Japan (late period) | "Fukoku kyōhei" rising narrative from 1850s, fails with Boshin War? | Terminal collapse very fast (15 y from Perry to Meiji) – consistent |
| Khedival Egypt (1860s‑1882) | Ismail's rising narrative ("My country is no longer in Africa, it is in Europe"), financial collapse and British occupation | Fast loss of sovereignty (6 y from default to occupation) – consistent |

If these cases follow the predicted speed pattern, the hypothesis could be generalised to **modernising empires facing common‑mode external pressure**.

---

## 5. Cases that would **falsify** or **bound** the hypothesis

The hypothesis would be weakened if:

- A case with a clear public rising narrative and a major visible failure event **survived for more than 50 years** without narrative retraction.
- A case with a declining narrative from the start **collapsed in ≤ 30 years** from the point of stress onset, contradicting the slow‑speed expectation.

Potential falsification candidates:

- **Russian Empire (1905‑1917)** – rising narrative of Stolypin reforms after 1905, failure in WWI, fast collapse (12 y) – actually *supports* FRH.
- **Brazilian Empire (1889)** – rising narrative after 1870s (abolition, modernisation), military coup, fast collapse (2 y) – supports.
- **Kingdom of Italy (1922)** – rising narrative of liberal Italy, failure in WWI and Fiume, collapse to fascism – complex, maybe supports.

A genuine falsification would be a case where a regime publicly promised renewal, suffered a catastrophic symbol‑failure, continued to promise renewal without retraction, and yet endured for generations. Such a case has not yet been identified.

## 6. Confidence statement for the synthesis

**Current confidence: Level C (plausible, limited testing).**
The hypothesis is logically coherent, supported by a strong within‑case replication (Ottoman young‑Turk cycle), and consistent with the Qing‑Tang speed differential. However, the number of fully coded cases is small (2 direct comparison, 1 boundary). The synthesis should present the hypothesis as a **provisional finding** with an explicit commitment to test against further cases, not as an established law.

**Recommendation:** In the May 15 synthesis, state the hypothesis as a **working tool** for making falsifiable predictions, not as a confirmed signature. The synthesis can claim that "the available evidence is consistent with the hypothesis; the project's next phase will test it against the Habsburg, Soviet, and Tokugawa cases."
