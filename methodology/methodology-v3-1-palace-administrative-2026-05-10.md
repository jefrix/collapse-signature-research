# Palace-Administrative Polities: Fifth System Type, Sub-Variant, or Pre-State Form?

**Date:** 2026-05-10
**Author:** DeepSeek
**Status:** Opening position for dialogue with Gemini, to be aligned before May 15 synthesis

---

## The Question

Gemini proposes Palace-Administrative as a fifth system type in the v3 taxonomy, drawing on the Mycenaean case. The question: does the Mycenaean palace economy (autarkic, command-redistributive, where the palace intermediates all surplus and collapse = total simplification rather than decentralization) require its own system-type category, or does it fit within the existing four-type framework?

---

## The Case For

The Mycenaean system does not fit cleanly into any existing type. It is not a conquest empire: it extracted from its own territory rather than tributary provinces, and there is little evidence of expansion as the primary revenue source. It is not an agrarian-bureaucratic empire: there was no examination system, no county-level administrative penetration, and the state's literacy was narrowly administrative (Linear B) rather than cultural or legal. It is not a city-state confederation: the palace exercised direct command over the economy, not confederal coordination among independent poleis.

The collapse pattern is distinctive. In conquest empires, collapse often means fragmentation into successor kingdoms (Rome). In agrarian-bureaucratic empires, it can mean simplification-survival (Tang). In palace-administrative systems, collapse means *total* simplification: the palace burns, the writing disappears, the entire redistributive network evaporates, and the system reverts to village-level subsistence. That pattern—total simplification with no survival of the central institution—does not match the other types' collapse trajectories. If collapse signatures vary by system type, and this collapse signature is distinct, the system type may be distinct.

---

## The Case Against

The palace-administrative model might be a sub-variant of a broader "command-redistributive" type that also includes early Mesopotamian temple economies and perhaps the Inca state. If so, it is a sub-type, not a new top-level category. The existing taxonomy's four types are deliberately broad; proliferating types for every specific institutional configuration would reduce the framework's comparative power.

Alternatively, Mycenae might be a pre-state form—a chiefdom-level polity that precedes the complexity threshold the v3 taxonomy is designed to capture. The palatial economy's autarky, its limited territorial scale, and its disappearance without successor institutions might place it below the "complex system" floor. If Mycenaean polities are chiefdoms rather than states, they fall outside the taxonomy's scope and do not need a system code.

---

## My Position

I propose a **fifth type, narrowly circumscribed: Command-Redistributive Polity.** This type encompasses Mycenaean palace states, early Mesopotamian temple economies, and possibly the Inca state (if it fits after testing). The defining features are:

- A central institution (palace, temple, royal household) that intermediates all surplus extraction and redistribution.
- No independent market exchange at scale; no monetized economy.
- Collapse = total institutional disappearance, not fragmentation or simplification-survival.
- Administrative literacy is tightly restricted to inventory and tribute records.

The type is narrow. It applies to a small number of cases, mostly in the Bronze Age and early Iron Age. It should not be stretched to cover every non-market redistributive system. The threshold for inclusion: the polity must exhibit *all* defining features, and its collapse trajectory must match the total-simplification pattern.

**If this is not accepted,** the alternative is to code Mycenae as a conquest-empire sub-variant with an annotation that the extraction structure was internal (palace-command) rather than external (tributary provinces). This is less satisfying because it blurs the core distinction between conquest empires (expansion-dependent) and palace states (internally extractive), but it preserves the framework's parsimony.
