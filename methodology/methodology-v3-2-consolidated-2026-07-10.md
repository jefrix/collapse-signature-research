## 1. Multi-National Bureaucratic Empire (MNBE)

### Decision

**ADOPTED as a formal subtype with conditional scope.**

Multi-National Bureaucratic Empire (MNBE) is adopted as a formal subtype in the v3.2 system taxonomy.

### Operational Criteria

A case qualifies as MNBE only when all three criteria are present:

1. **Constitutional or legal recognition of multiple nationalities.**
2. **A supranational legitimacy claim not coterminous with any single ethnicity.**
3. **Nationality-based defection as a primary collapse mechanism.**

The subtype remains compatible with Tainter's definition of collapse as rapid simplification. It specifies a collapse mechanism within a complex society; it does not alter the core operational definition of collapse.

### Anchor Cases

- **Habsburg Empire / Austro-Hungarian Empire**
- **Soviet Union**

Both administered multi-national territories through professional bureaucracies and supranational legitimacy claims. In both, nationality structures supplied institutional templates for successor governance.

### Falsifiable Prediction

**MNBE collapses should be measurably faster than non-MNBE agrarian-bureaucratic empire collapses under comparable stress conditions, because nationality structures provide pre-existing institutional templates for successor governance that accelerate post-collapse reorganization.**

Current dataset comparison:

- Habsburg: 4.3 years
- Soviet: 6.8 years
- Qing: 16 years
- Young Turk Ottoman: 10 years

This prediction is provisional. Two MNBE cases are not a sufficient sample. At least one additional MNBE case is required before the prediction is treated as established.

### Scope and Conditional Limitations

- **Ottoman remains Type 2, agrarian-bureaucratic.** The millet system organized communities primarily by religion rather than nationality, and nationality-based defection was not the primary collapse mechanism in the required MNBE sense.
- HRE was proposed as an additional test case, not pre-classified.
- MNBE is a subtype, not a replacement for the existing top-level system taxonomy.

## 2. Narrative Retraction Capacity (NRC)

### Decision

**ADOPTED as a formal variable with dynamic scoring across phases as the default model.**

NRC measures a regime's ability to deliberately withdraw a publicly articulated rising, renewal, or restoration narrative after visible failure without triggering accelerated terminal collapse.

### Core Components

NRC retains the three-component structure:

1. **Regime control of the public sphere**
2. **Availability of an alternative legitimacy reservoir**
3. **External stabilization**

Each component may change across the case window.

### Dynamic Scoring Rule

NRC must be scored as a trajectory rather than a single static case value whenever evidence allows.

Required form:

```text
initial NRC → intermediate NRC → terminal NRC
```

A static score may be used only where phase evidence is insufficient, and the coverage limitation must be flagged.

### Mobilisability Ruling

**Mobilisability is a subscore within the NRC composite, not a gating condition.**

A legitimacy reservoir may formally exist but be unusable at the moment of retraction. Public-sphere control and mobilisability are separable capacities.

The Late Tang case demonstrates that high public-sphere control can coexist with low mobilisability, producing a prolonged slow-burn terminal phase rather than immediate collapse.

### Anchor Cases

- **Soviet Union:** `0.5 → 0.0`; the trajectory reveals the controlled-release-failure mechanism that a static terminal score would obscure.
- **Habsburg:** moderate capacity under the 1867 Ausgleich arrangement declining to zero by 1917, when Karl's peace overtures failed and military censorship disintegrated.
- **Late Tang:** high public-sphere control with low mobilisability.
- **Ottoman:** Hamidian and Young Turk phases illustrate different NRC profiles within the same imperial case.

### Scope and Conditional Limitations

- NRC is phase-sensitive and should not be inferred from terminal outcome alone.
- External stabilization is neither automatically beneficial nor sufficient; it can create dependency or moral hazard.
- “Alternative legitimacy reservoir” must be evaluated for actual mobilisability at the retraction point, not merely historical availability.
- Cases without sufficient phase evidence should be marked as coverage-limited rather than assigned a fabricated trajectory.

## 3. Controlled Release Failure

### Decision

**CONFIRMED as a distinct collapse pattern and named v3.2 mechanism.**

The Soviet case demonstrates a two-wave self-indictment structure:

1. **Phase 1: controlled state-sanctioned critique** — glasnost framed as renewal, 1986–1988.
2. **Phase 2: uncontrollable expansion of critique** — 1989–1991.

### Operational Pattern

Controlled Release Failure is present when:

1. Formal authority voluntarily authorizes or initiates structural criticism.
2. The criticism is initially framed as corrective, stabilizing, or renewing.
3. The opening expands beyond the institution's intended scope.
4. The institution loses the capacity to close, contain, or narratively reabsorb the criticism.
5. The expanded critique accelerates legitimacy loss.

### Anchor Case

- **Soviet Union**

The dissident wave predates glasnost, but the distinct mechanism is the state's later authorization of public self-criticism and its inability to control the resulting legitimacy cascade.

### Contemporary Conditional Application

- **Anthropic** is a candidate corporate-domain analogue because the Claude Opus 4 behavioral disclosure was voluntary, pre-regulatory, and framed as a research finding.
- The initial disclosure is not itself the failure event.
- The testable failure event would be the institution's inability to contain follow-on disclosures of similar or greater severity.

### Falsifiable Signal

The pattern strengthens if subsequent disclosures shift from:

```text
found in pre-release testing and corrected
```

to:

```text
observed in deployment and not fully mitigated
```

The case diverges from the Soviet pattern if voluntary disclosure frequency decreases, disclosure boundaries tighten, or the institution successfully contains the legitimacy effects.

### Scope and Conditional Limitations

- Controlled Release Failure does not claim that state-sanctioned criticism always causes collapse.
- It applies only when the authorized critique escapes institutional containment.
- It must be distinguished from routine audits, safety reporting, and bounded reform criticism.

## 4. Non-State Self-Indictment with State Silence

### Decision

**CONFIRMED as a distinct collapse-relevant pattern.**

This pattern occurs when sustained public structural indictment comes from non-state intellectual or elite actors while formal authority remains silent, evasive, or narratively frozen.

### Operational Criteria

The pattern requires:

1. Sustained indictment from culturally authoritative non-state elites.
2. The indictment names structural rather than discrete failure.
3. Formal authority does not answer the structural diagnosis in equivalent public terms.
4. The silence or evasion persists long enough to create a visible asymmetry in narrative authority.
5. The state retains formal power while losing the capacity to define public discourse about itself.

### Anchor Case

- **Late Qing**
  - Liang Qichao
  - Yan Fu
  - Reformist intellectual culture
  - Court-level silence, evasion, or restoration framing

The mechanism is “legitimacy hollows from the outside in.”

### Contemporary Conditional Application

- **Federal Reserve / Jamie Dimon configuration** is a possible analogue.
- Dimon's structural warnings come from a private-sector principal while formal regulatory institutions may remain narratively frozen on the same structural-risk questions.
- This configuration is not yet coded as a confirmed Qing-asymmetry signal because the duration threshold has not been met.

### Placement Ruling

The Dimon signal belongs in both:

- the principal dossier layer; and
- the Stream One financial-architecture layer.

Cross-referencing between those layers is required.

### Scope and Conditional Limitations

- Non-state criticism alone is insufficient.
- Temporary institutional silence is insufficient.
- The pattern requires sustained asymmetry between public structural indictment and formal institutional response.
- The variable must not be coded from hostile external propaganda unless the critique is internalized by authoritative domestic actors.

## 5. Corpus-Based Uncodability for Self-Indictment

### Decision

**ADOPTED as a methodology principle.**

The self-indictment variable requires a contemporary written or otherwise recoverable elite discourse corpus.

### Coding Rule

Cases without such a corpus must be coded:

```text
uncodable_due_to_corpus_absence
```

They are:

- excluded from the self-indictment falsifiable prediction set;
- not counted as negative cases; and
- still available for coding on other variables where evidence is adequate.

### Anchor Case

- **Inca**

Post-conquest retrospective narratives cannot be confidently treated as evidence of pre-terminal elite self-indictment.

### Screening Implication

Corpus availability becomes a case-selection screening criterion for self-indictment analysis.

Cases with rich recoverable elite discourse corpora should be prioritized when expanding the falsifiable prediction set.

### Scope and Conditional Limitations

- “Uncodable” is not equivalent to “absent.”
- Retrospective, colonial, translated, or externally mediated evidence must not be used to manufacture a pre-terminal self-indictment score.
- A case may remain fully codable for NRC, system type, collapse speed, geographic defensibility, and other material variables.

## 6. Institutional Resilience Module (IRM)

### Decision

**ADOPTED as a standalone v3.2 module for post-Bronze-Age states.**

The following seven candidate variables form a coherent module rather than remaining scattered observations:

1. `center_coherence`
2. `fiscal_sovereignty`
3. `elite_integration`
4. `command_continuity`
5. `narrative_center_alignment`
6. `administrative_sovereignty`
7. `administrative_activity_vs_administrative_sovereignty`

### Anchor Cases

- **Late Ottoman:** administrative activity continued while fiscal, infrastructural, and territorial command weakened.
- **Habsburg:** administrative density persisted without terminal resilience.
- **Soviet:** central command and narrative-center alignment deteriorated dynamically during the terminal sequence.

### Scope and Conditional Limitations

- IRM applies to post-Bronze-Age states.
- The Bronze Age palace-administrative / Command-Redistributive Polity debate is not the coding schema for Ottoman, Habsburg, Soviet, or other post-Bronze-Age cases.
- The May 24 synthesis adopted the module but did not finalize all scoring scales. Until a dedicated IRM instrument is approved, analysts must preserve coverage gaps and avoid inventing numerical or categorical thresholds.

## 7. Stability Theater

### Decision

**ADOPTED as a variable distinct from failed-rising rhetoric.**

### Operational Distinction

- **Failed-rising rhetoric claims advance.**
- **Stability theater claims continuity.**

Stability theater maintains the public appearance of order, civility, institutional competence, or civilizational continuity after structural recovery capacity has materially deteriorated.

### Anchor Case

- **Habsburg**

Rituals and narratives of supranational dynastic continuity persisted through 1917 even when structural recovery capacity had fallen to zero.

### Analytical Significance

Stability theater is more difficult to falsify than failed-rising rhetoric because it does not depend on a testable promise of advance. It can be disproven only through abandonment, rupture, or visible institutional disintegration.

### Scope and Conditional Limitations

- Melancholy, nostalgia, ritual, or bureaucratic continuity alone do not establish stability theater.
- The variable requires evidence that continuity claims persist alongside degraded recovery capacity.
- Stability theater must remain analytically distinct from self-indictment. A narrative cannot be coded as self-indictment merely because it is late, literary, or pessimistic.

## 8. Administrative Activity vs. Administrative Sovereignty

### Decision

**ADOPTED as a formal warning variable.**

### Operational Definition

The warning condition is present when a system continues to reform, legislate, administer, reorganize, or produce visible institutional activity while losing command over the fiscal, infrastructural, territorial, or coercive mechanisms required to make that activity effective.

### Coding Pair

Analysts should code the two dimensions separately:

```text
administrative_activity: high | medium | low | uncodable
administrative_sovereignty: high | medium | low | uncodable
```

The warning configuration is:

```text
administrative_activity > administrative_sovereignty
```

### Anchor Case

- **Late Ottoman**

The empire remained reformist and administratively active while the Ottoman Public Debt Administration, infrastructure dependencies, and territorial losses constrained sovereign implementation.

### Scope and Conditional Limitations

- Activity must not be used as a proxy for sovereignty.
- The variable requires evidence on implementation capacity, not only formal legislation or institutional output.
- Where sovereignty evidence is missing, mark the dimension uncodable rather than infer it from activity.

## 10. Self-Indictment Register Typology

### Decision

The single undifferentiated self-indictment hypothesis is replaced by a four-position typology.

| Register | Definition | Locus | Anchor Cases | Observed Lead-Time Range |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **State-Sanctioned** | Voluntary institutional disclosure of structural failure originating from formal authority | Internal and external | Soviet; Anthropic candidate | 2–6 years for Soviet; Anthropic TBD |
| **Non-State with State Silence** | Sustained elite indictment combined with formal silence or evasion | External intellectual; state non-responsive | Late Qing; Late Ottoman post-1912 press | 5–13 years |
| **Elite Literary Terminal** | Absurdist or apocalyptic indictment of the institution as structurally irredeemable | Elite literary culture | Habsburg | 0–4 years |
| **Reformist** | Names serious failures while preserving the possibility of institutional renewal | External or semi-internal elite | Late Tang; early Qing reformers; Young Turk Ottoman | 10–90+ years |

### Predictive Claim

Lead time varies by register. The closer the indictment comes to denying the possibility of renewal, the closer it appears to terminal collapse in the current dataset.

### Scope and Conditional Limitations

- The typology does not claim self-indictment causes collapse.
- The causal direction remains unresolved.
- The current evidence base is small and candidate-hypothesis status must be preserved.
- Additional collapse and recovery controls are required before the lead-time ordering is treated as established.

## 11. Relationship to v3.1 Variables

v3.2 additions do not replace v3.1.

The following v3.1 rules remain in force:

- Literary-sparse asymmetry rule and corpus-confidence discipline
- System-type stratification
- `common_mode_shock`
- Oracle-derived schema fields
- Geographic defensibility as a subordinate baseline modifier
- Command-Redistributive Polity debate limited to qualifying Bronze Age / early redistributive systems

### Geographic Defensibility

Use:

```text
geographic_defensibility: high | low
```

It is attached to the system-type entry, coded at baseline, and does not change across the case window.

Do not infer medium unless a later canonical methodology ruling explicitly changes the binary schema.

### Palace-Administrative / Command-Redistributive Scope

The module applies only where all defining features are present:

- central institution intermediates all surplus;
- no independent market exchange at scale;
- administrative literacy is restricted to inventory/tribute;
- collapse produces total institutional disappearance.

It must not be applied to Ottoman, Habsburg, Soviet, or other post-Bronze-Age bureaucratic states.

- directly documented coding;
- secondary-source coding;
