## Observations

1. **Elite self‑indictment is present in all cases where a rich literary record exists**, though its register varies dramatically – from the restrained Confucian polemic of Han Yu to the scorching absurdist indictment of Kraus.

2. **The Habsburg case is the strongest**, with self‑indictment produced by central literary figures during the collapse itself, in a tone that leaves no room for regime salvage. This may be a unique feature of cases where an intellectual elite has already internalised the state’s illegitimacy before the formal dissolution.

3. **The Qing case shows asymmetry**: self‑indictment is vibrant among non‑state intellectuals but absent from the court itself. This asymmetry may be a specific mechanism: the state loses the argument even while still technically governing.

4. **The Soviet case demonstrates that state‑sanctioned self‑indictment can accelerate collapse** rather than serve as a safety valve. Once glasnost permitted public criticism, the legitimacy of the entire system crumbled.

5. **The cross‑case evidence is consistent with the hypothesis that elite self‑indictment functions as a leading indicator**, appearing before or during the terminal sequence in all six cases where it can be assessed. The hypothesis should be tested against additional non‑collapse control cases (e.g., the Ming recovery, early Meiji Japan) to determine whether self‑indictment is specific to collapse or also appears in recovery‑phase literature.

6. **Inca is uncodable** due to the absence of a pre‑conquest written corpus. This limitation must be noted in the methodology: the self‑indictment hypothesis can only be tested on cases with a robust contemporary literary record.
