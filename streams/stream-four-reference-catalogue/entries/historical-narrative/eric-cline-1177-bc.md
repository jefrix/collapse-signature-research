# *1177 B.C.: The Year Civilization Collapsed* – Eric H. Cline

- **Title:** *1177 B.C.: The Year Civilization Collapsed*
- **Author:** Eric H. Cline
- **Date:** 2014 (first edition)
- **Type:** historical-narrative (non‑fiction)
- **Source location:** Princeton University Press. Project copy in Stream Three source materials.
- **Load‑bearing passage:** Cline’s central narrative – that the Late Bronze Age collapse was not a single invasion but a “perfect storm” of interconnected system failures (earthquakes, drought, famine, invaders, trade disruption) – provides a **structural metaphor** for systemic interdependence as a vulnerability. The specific image of the Ugarit letters (RS 18.147, the king of Ugarit writing “the enemy ships are here, my cities are burning…” while also noting that his own army and navy were elsewhere, dispatched to help the Hittites) compresses the tragedy of systemic interdependence into a single document.
- **Analytical use:** Cline’s framework is the project’s primary illustration of how high systemic integration can become a collapse accelerator rather than a resilience feature. The Ugarit letter is load‑bearing because it shows a ruler who failed not from incompetence but from having *fulfilled his alliance obligations* – the system killed him because it was too integrated. This directly informs the Stream One analysis of the AI circular‑investment web: each participant’s rational commitment to the system makes the aggregate more fragile.
