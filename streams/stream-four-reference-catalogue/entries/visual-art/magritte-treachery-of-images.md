# *The Treachery of Images* (La Trahison des images) – René Magritte

- **Title:** *The Treachery of Images* (La Trahison des images)
- **Author:** René Magritte (1898–1967)
- **Date:** 1929 (painted)
- **Type:** visual-art
- **Source location:** Los Angeles County Museum of Art (LACMA). Reproductions widely available; project may reference under fair use for analytical purposes.
- **Load‑bearing passage:** The painting depicts a pipe with the caption “Ceci n’est pas une pipe” (This is not a pipe). The load‑bearing element is the **conceptual destabilisation** of the relationship between representation and reality: the painting is not a pipe, it is an image of a pipe. The caption is true in a way that undermines the viewer’s default trust in representation.
- **Analytical use:** Directly mapped to the project’s method of reading official documents (e.g., Federal Reserve FSR, IMF GFSR, technocratic manifestos) as representations that may not correspond to operational reality. The painting compresses the argument that “the official description of the system is not the system” into an instantly graspable visual. Use when the prose needs to destabilise a reader’s trust in an official narrative without launching a lengthy deconstruction.
- **Project context:** Recommended in the project onboarding document’s cultural‑frame catalogue under “For meaning, pattern, and what exceeds materialism.” Applicable to financial dispatches (the gap between CRE valuations and actual market prices), technocratic‑class analysis (the gap between “AI will benefit everyone” and the capital flows), and historical case studies (the gap between late‑period imperial ideology and structural reality).
- **Catalogue notes:** The reference is well‑known; use with precision, not as a generic “things are not what they seem.” The power is in the specific mechanism: the painting is not a pipe, but it is also not *not* a pipe. The same ambiguity should be preserved in the prose application.
