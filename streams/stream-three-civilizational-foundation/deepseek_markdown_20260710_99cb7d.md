### NRC Dynamic Arc

The Habsburg Empire’s Narrative Retraction Capacity was not static. It changed significantly between the *Ausgleich* (1867) and the terminal dissolution (1918).

| Phase | Regime Control of Public Sphere | Alternative Legitimacy Reservoir | External Stabilisation | NRC Score |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ausgleich era (1867–1908)** | Medium‑high. Press was relatively free but could be muzzled in emergencies; dualist structure limited censorship in Hungary. | Dynastic Catholicism still a meaningful reservoir, especially for the German‑Austrian elite and conservative Slavs. | None formal, but the European balance of power acted as an informal stabilizer; no power actively sought to dismember Austria. | **2.0 (moderate)** |
| **Pre‑war crisis (1908–1914)** | Declining. Parliament paralyzed by nationalist obstruction; press increasingly nationalist. | Dynastic legitimacy waning; Franz Joseph personally respected but the institution eroding. | Alliance with Germany provided military backing but also dependency; no economic stabilization. | **1.5 (declining)** |
| **War years (1914–1916)** | Further decline. Military censorship imposed but became inconsistent; casualty news and food shortages undermined official narrative. | Catholic universalism could not compete with war‑driven nationalisms; dynasty’s authority increasingly confined to the army. | German military support kept the empire afloat, but the relationship shifted to subordination; no economic floor. | **1.0 (low)** |
| **Visibility break (1916–1917)** | Collapsing. Reichsrat reconvened; national councils operated openly; Emperor Karl’s peace feelers leaked. | None. Karl’s attempt to appeal to a supranational federalism failed; national councils rejected it. | Germany remained a military prop but could not stabilize the home front; no allied financial support. | **0.5 (very low)** |
| **October 1917 – November 1918** | Zero. Censorship broke down completely; national armies disintegrated. | None. The dynasty’s last manifesto (16 Oct 1918) was ignored. | None. German collapse imminent; no external actor willing to preserve the empire. | **0.0 (absent)** |

**Interpretation:** The NRC score moved from a moderate 2.0 in the *Ausgleich* era, where retraction might have been feasible (if a major failure had occurred), to a terminal 0.0 by late 1917, making any retraction or recovery impossible. The failure of Karl’s 1918 manifesto illustrates this: even a modest retraction (federalization) was rejected because the regime no longer controlled the public sphere and had no credible legitimacy reservoir to invoke. The Habsburg arc thus serves as a benchmark for dynamic NRC scoring and confirms the v3.2 ruling that NRC must be assessed temporally, not as a single static value.
