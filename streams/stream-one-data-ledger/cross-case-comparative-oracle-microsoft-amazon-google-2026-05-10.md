## Executive Summary

This comparative freezes the four committed Stream One cases as a single synthesis set: O = [Oracle](https://www.oracle.com), M = [Microsoft](https://www.microsoft.com), A = [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com), and G = [Alphabet](https://abc.xyz) / [Google](https://about.google). Read side by side, the set now supports four distinct fragility subtypes rather than one generic "AI infrastructure risk" category: externally mediated / vehicle-heavy fragility for O, on-balance equity-platform fragility for M, self-funded platform fragility for A, and vertically integrated chokepoint fragility for G. The core comparative result is that the v3 schema does discriminate meaningfully across the four cases, especially on actor-role complexity, obligation recognition state, counterparty concentration visibility, power dependence, and common-mode shock susceptibility.

The main caution is overfit risk. Two v3 fields remain highly informative but currently narrow in empirical range: `customer_funding_mode` is strongly active in O and largely dormant in the other three, while `guarantee_support` is materially active in O and G but not in M or A. That does not make those fields useless. It does mean they should be treated as selective discriminators rather than universal signature variables until a broader case set confirms whether O was exceptional or merely early.

## Fragility Subtypes

**O: externally mediated / vehicle-heavy fragility.** O's subtype is defined by the fact that the growth story is real, but a meaningful part of the exposure is mediated through structures outside the clean, ordinary issuer balance sheet: signed but not yet commenced leases, lessor borrowing, customer prepayments, customer-supplied GPUs, and a demand profile tightly linked to OAI/Stargate. The resulting fragility is not simple insolvency risk. It is dependence on external vehicles, counterparties, and infrastructure commitments whose interaction can turn apparent scale into compensatory fragility if one channel breaks.

**M: on-balance equity-platform fragility.** M's subtype is less vehicle-driven and more balance-sheet-integrated. Its exposure is organized around a long-term strategic partnership with OAI, revenue-sharing arrangements, OpenAI IP and Azure API exclusivity, rights of first refusal on new capacity, and continued datacenter buildout on M's own platform. The fragility here is therefore not primarily hidden-vehicle fragility. It is concentration inside a large, resilient balance sheet and a tightly integrated platform-investment arrangement that can still transmit a common shock through revenue, capex, capacity allocation, and strategic IP dependence at the same time.

**A: self-funded platform fragility.** A remains the lowest-disclosed-risk benchmark in the current four-case set because the validated case is dominated by self-funded infrastructure expansion, AWS platform economics, and a broad service model rather than by visible project-vehicle engineering. The relevant risk is still real: A depends on continued demand for AI services, large and rising infrastructure spend, and the ability to scale custom silicon and platform capacity for external labs and customers. But the disclosed fragility sits mainly on the issuer's own platform and cash-generation capacity, not in an externally mediated financing stack.

**G: vertically integrated chokepoint fragility.** G is the opposite of Oracle-style external mediation. Its configuration internalizes the frontier lab, custom chips, cloud platform, model deployment, and major external-lab exposure into one stack. That lowers one class of fragility — dependence on third-party merchant compute — but raises another by concentrating risk in the TPU-power-cloud complex and then layering large external investment and backlog exposure on top of it. The fragility therefore comes from endogenous chokepoint concentration: when the same compute, power, and capacity system feeds internal frontier-building, cloud revenue, and external-lab commitments, one shock can travel through all of them at once.

## Historical Chokepoint Analogs

These analogs are heuristic, not identity claims. Their purpose is to anchor the contemporary cases in the historical language already emerging from Stream Three: provisioning chokepoints, allocative chokepoints, and network chokepoints.

| Case | Closest historical analog | Why this is the best fit |
|---|---|---|
| O | Late Tang Grand Canal | Rising throughput can conceal rising dependence. O's expanding contracted volume resembles a canal logic in which more flow is not automatically more resilience. |
| M | Mycenaean palace ledger | M's exposure is centrally organized through administrative rights, platform control, and allocative authority rather than through a visible external vehicle chain. |
| A | Late Bronze Age trade-network dependency | A's frozen case read is the most network-platform-like: broad service intermediation, multi-partner model availability, and lower disclosed dependence on one financing vehicle. |
| G | Western Rome's African grain dependence | G's TPU-power-cloud complex functions like a provisioning core feeding a larger imperial platform. The issue is concentrated provisioning, not merely high spending. |

The historical basis for these analogs is strong enough for provisional use. Western Rome's late imperial West became increasingly sensitive to the African grain supply, while the Grand Canal was the artery carrying surplus grain from the Yangtze and Huai to northern capitals and armies; after the An Lushan rupture, the Tang court became increasingly dependent on the Huai–Yangtze economic heartland and the canal system rebuilt and financed through the salt monopoly. In the current corporate set, O most clearly resembles a corridor-dependent provisioning story, M a centrally administered allocative story, A a network-intermediation story, and G a provisioning-core story.

## High-Signal Fields and Overfit Risk

A useful rule of thumb after four cases is straightforward. A field is **high-signal** if it is materially non-null in most cases **and** changes the classification. A field is **overfit-risk** if it is vivid but currently fires in only one case.

| Signal class | Fields | Why |
|---|---|---|
| High-signal | `actor_legal_role_split`; `obligation_recognition_state`; `counterparty_concentration_visibility`; `common_mode_shock_susceptibility` | These four fields matter in every case and are doing real classification work rather than decorative work. |
| Medium-signal | `vehicle_visibility`; `power_dependency_obligation`; `guarantee_support` | These fields do not fire equally everywhere, but when they fire they materially change the read. |
| Overfit-risk for now | `customer_funding_mode` | Strongly discriminating in O, weak or near-null elsewhere in the current set. |

The practical conclusion is that the schema is holding, but unevenly. The role split, obligation state, concentration visibility, and common-mode shock fields are already "core comparative" variables. Vehicle visibility and guarantee support look real but still situational. Customer funding mode should be retained, because O shows why it matters, but it should be treated as a currently narrow discriminator until another case activates it in a similarly consequential way.
