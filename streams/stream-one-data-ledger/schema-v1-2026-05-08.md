Pilot framing
The Stream One pilot should treat the AI capital web, the CRE maturity wall, and the private-credit / private-equity transmission layer as one linked architecture rather than three adjacent topics. The current official-source picture supports that design. The IMF’s April 2026 GFSR says global financial stability risks are elevated and emphasizes amplification channels, including short-term issuance dependence, cross-border portfolio flows mediated by nonbanks, and market-to-balance-sheet spillovers. The March 2026 BIS material adds two key pieces directly relevant to this pilot: AI infrastructure is increasingly financed through debt, including off-balance-sheet “shadow borrowing” structures with private credit and bank support lines; and private credit exposures tied to software and AI vulnerability have become large enough to matter as an opacity and redemption-risk channel. The FDIC’s Q4 2025 Banking Profile shows a banking system that remains profitable and liquid overall but still faces elevated unrealized losses and persistent weakness in some CRE portfolios, especially non-owner-occupied CRE. The OFR’s 2025 Annual Report adds further linkage points: technology and cyber concentration, office-CRE mortgage risk, rapidly rising hedge-fund borrowing, and broader leverage and credit weakness in leveraged loans and private lending. 

The pilot should therefore not begin by collecting facts ad hoc. It should begin by fixing a schema that can represent five things at once: who the actors are, what instruments connect them, where the maturities sit, what dependencies create hidden fragility, and how stress could transmit from AI financing structures into banks, nonbanks, or CRE refinancing channels. The schema below is built to do exactly that.

This schema deliberately carries the compensatory-fragility logic over from Stream Three because the same analytic problem appears in contemporary finance. A rising financing volume can mean growth, but it can also mean substitution for internal cash generation, balance-sheet evasion, or dependency concentration. The BIS’s January and March 2026 AI-financing work is especially important here: it describes a shift from cash-flow financing to debt, notes that off-balance-sheet AI infrastructure vehicles can amount to “shadow borrowing,” and says those structures strengthen links between large tech borrowers, private credit, insurers, and banks through funding lines and guarantees. That is exactly the kind of architecture where surface expansion and hidden fragility can move together.

The BIS layer deserves to be split internally when the ledger is implemented, because the March 2026 issue contains several pilot-relevant components rather than one undifferentiated signal. The overview chapter says credit markets stayed broadly stable but that private dealmaking and leveraged-loan activity softened and concerns over AI-driven disruption refocused attention on private-credit portfolios. One box explains that AI infrastructure build-outs are increasingly funded with long-dated debt and off-balance-sheet structures backed by private credit and institutional lenders, with banks supporting the vehicles through funding lines. Another says private credit lending to software firms exceeded $500 billion by end-2025 and that software-sector repricing fed into business-development-company performance and investor concern. A separate box says banks’ cross-border claims on nonbanks rose much faster than overall cross-border lending, taking that share from 7% in 2015 to 24% in 2025. Those are not side notes; they are the architecture of the pilot. 

The FDIC and OFR layers are likewise complementary rather than redundant. The FDIC provides the best standardized quarterly snapshot of insured-bank profitability, deposits, reserve coverage, problem-bank counts, longer-duration assets, unrealized securities losses, and especially portfolio-level CRE delinquency and nonaccrual trends. In Q4 2025 it said non-owner-occupied CRE PDNA rates remained elevated, especially at very large banks, and that unrealized losses, though lower, remained elevated. The OFR’s 2025 report adds systemwide interpretation: technology and cyber concentration, office-building mortgage weakness, investor optimism in corporate credit and equities, rapid growth in hedge-fund borrowing, and the growing scale of leveraged loans and private lending. For this pilot, FDIC tells you how the regulated-bank interface looks quarter by quarter; OFR tells you which broader linkages deserve monitoring around that interface. 

Minimum pilot fields to populate first
The first round of population should be narrow. It should fill only the fields required to make the AI-CRE-PE linkage analyzable:

First-pass field set	Why it comes first
actor_registry	prevents scattered findings about unnamed institutions
instrument_registry	distinguishes debt, lease, guarantee, and support-line structures
maturity_wall	anchors the “wall” concept in actual dates and resets
dependency_topology	identifies concentration and chokepoints early
compensatory_fragility	prevents misreading volume growth as resilience
transmission_channels	defines how AI finance and CRE stress meet
source_and_confidence	keeps every entry auditable from day one

That first-pass discipline matters because the source stack already warns against naive interpretation. The IMF is emphasizing amplification channels, the BIS is documenting shadow-borrowing style AI structures and expanding bank-nonbank linkages, the FDIC is showing persistent CRE weakness within an otherwise resilient banking sector, and the OFR is highlighting both office-CRE and private-credit or hedge-fund vulnerability. If the ledger starts as a list of interesting facts instead of a structured exposure map, it will reproduce exactly the scattered-findings problem the project wants to avoid.
