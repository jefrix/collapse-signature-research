## 1. CORPUS SCOPE AND SELECTION RATIONALE

The Late Tang literary corpus is the richest body of contemporaneous collapse literature in the premodern dataset. No other case (except possibly the late Habsburg) has multiple first-person witnesses of collapse producing work of canonical stature during the collapse itself. The selection below privileges:

- **Contemporaneous production** (written during or immediately after the An Lushan Rebellion and its fiscal aftermath, c. 755–810 CE)
- **Direct engagement with the collapse** (not simply late-period literature that happens to date from after 755)
- **Multiple genres** (poetry, prose, institutional writing, fiction) to capture different registers of the collapse experience

The corpus is organized by author with thematic coding attached to each excerpt.

## 2. DU FU (杜甫, 712–770): THE COLLAPSE WITNESS

Du Fu is the central figure. He was 43 when the rebellion broke out and lived through the occupation of Chang'an, the flight of the court, and the devastated post-rebellion landscape. His war poetry is the Tang case's closest equivalent to a first-person collapse narrative.

### 2.1 "Spring Prospect" (春望, 757)

**Context:** Written in Chang'an during rebel occupation. Du Fu had been captured (or trapped) in the city. The poem is eight lines of five-character regulated verse—the most compressed and demanding classical form.

**Chinese text (excerpt):**
> 國破山河在，城春草木深。
> 感時花濺淚，恨別鳥驚心。

**Translation:**
> The state is broken, but the hills and rivers remain;
> In spring, the city is choked with weeds and brambles.
> Feeling the times, flowers draw tears;
> Hating separation, birds alarm the heart.

**Thematic coding:**

| Variable | Coding | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Legitimacy narrative | **Collapsed.** The opening line ("state broken, hills and rivers remain") registers the destruction of political order against the endurance of the physical landscape. The political is contingent; the natural is permanent. | The line has become the canonical Chinese expression of state collapse ("國破山河在" is still used in modern Chinese to describe national catastrophe). |
| Golden-age nostalgia | **Implicit.** The "weeds and brambles" (草木深) on city streets implies the city's former order has been replaced by wild growth. | The contrast between civilization and encroaching wilderness is a collapse motif. |
| Center-periphery estrangement | **Acute.** The capital—the symbolic center—is experienced as alien and hostile territory. | Du Fu is in his own city as a prisoner. |
| Paralysis vs. agency | **Paralysis.** The speaker can only register emotion (tears, alarm); no action is possible. | The poem's structure moves from observation to emotional response to inaction. |
| Temporal mood | **Backward-looking.** The implied "before" (when the state was whole, when the city was ordered) haunts every line. | The poem works through implication rather than direct comparison. |

**Confidence: Level A.** Text is securely attributed and dated to 757 CE (or early 758 at latest). The reading is standard in Chinese literary scholarship.

### 2.2 "The Journey North" (北征, 757)

**Context:** Written after Du Fu escaped Chang'an and reached the exiled court at Fengxiang. The poem describes his return journey to his family in the north, through devastated countryside. Approximately 700 lines—unusually long for classical Chinese poetry and functioning as a prose-level documentary account in verse form.

**Key passage (lines describing the journey):**
> 夜深經戰場，寒月照白骨。
> ...
> 所遇多被傷，呻吟更流血。

**Translation (key lines):**
> Deep at night I pass the battlefield,
> Cold moon shining on white bones.
> ...
> Everywhere I encounter the wounded,
> Groaning, still bleeding.

**Thematic coding:**

| Variable | Coding | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Legitimacy narrative | **Collapsed.** The state cannot protect its people. The bodies are not heroic war dead; they are the abandoned casualties of a world that has stopped functioning. | The poem is a documentary record of what Du Fu actually saw, not what he imagined. |
| Elite self-indictment | **Present but implicit.** The elite decision to allow An Lushan to accumulate power is not named, but the consequences are laid out in exhaustive detail. | Du Fu does not assign individual blame; he documents results. |
| Displacement themes | **Central.** Du Fu is traveling through his own country as a refugee. The journey is a physical traversal of collapse. | The poem is organized around movement through ruined space. |
| Moral-order fracture | **Registered.** The poem describes not just death but the cessation of burial rites—the Confucian moral order literally cannot perform its basic functions. | "White bones" unburied = the most fundamental ritual failure in Confucian terms. |

**Confidence: Level A.** Text securely attributed and dated.

### 2.3 "Autumn Meditations" (秋興八首, 766)

**Context:** Written in Kuizhou (modern Chongqing), where Du Fu lived in relative safety after the rebellion. A sequence of eight regulated-verse poems reflecting on the fallen capital and the poet's own aging and displacement. The sequence is the most sustained meditation on collapse memory in the Chinese tradition.

**Key passage (Poem 4):**
> 聞道長安似弈棋，百年世事不勝悲。
> 王侯第宅皆新主，文武衣冠異昔時。

**Translation:**
> I hear Chang'an is like a chessboard,
> A hundred years of events—the grief cannot be borne.
> The mansions of princes and lords all have new masters now;
> The robes of civil and martial officials are not what they were.

**Thematic coding:**

| Variable | Coding | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Golden-age nostalgia | **Organizing and structural.** The entire sequence is built on the contrast between Chang'an-as-memory and Chang'an-as-rumor. The "chessboard" metaphor registers that the game has changed, the pieces rearranged, the old order gone. | This is the most elaborate treatment of collapse memory in the corpus. |
| Elite turnover | **Registered directly.** "Mansions of princes and lords all have new masters" is a direct observation of elite replacement. | Provides literary confirmation of the structural-demographic variable coded in the case brief. |
| Center-periphery estrangement | **Acute.** Du Fu is in Kuizhou, a remote Yangtze town, hearing about Chang'an through rumor ("I hear"). The center is inaccessible. | |
| Paralysis vs. agency | **Resigned acceptance.** The speaker can only remember and grieve; no political action is imagined. | Compares interestingly with Han Yu's active Confucian polemics a generation later. |
| Temporal mood | **Purely backward-looking.** The series moves from memory to memory; the "present" exists only as a diminished vantage point from which the past is viewed. | |

**Confidence: Level A.** Text securely attributed and dated to 766 CE.

### 2.4 "Dreaming of Li Bai" (夢李白二首, 758)

**Context:** Written when Du Fu believed his friend Li Bai had been executed for his involvement with the rebel Prince Lin's court. Li Bai had actually been exiled and survived. The poem operates as a dream-vision elegy for a friend presumed dead.

**Key lines:**
> 死別已吞聲，生別長惻惻。
> ...
> 恐非平生魂，路遠不可測。

**Translation:**
> Death-parting—I've swallowed my sobs;
> Life-parting—the grief lasts and lasts.
> ...
> I fear it is not your living soul I see,
> The road is too far, beyond all knowing.

**Thematic coding:**

| Variable | Coding | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Displacement themes | **Personal displacement stands for cultural loss.** Li Bai's presumed death represents the loss of the Kaiyuan-era cultural world that produced them both. | The poem works on literal and symbolic levels simultaneously. |
| Moral-order fracture | **Indirect.** The best poet of the age has been executed for a political misjudgment—the culture is devouring its own. | |

**Confidence: Level A.** Text securely attributed and dated to ~758 CE.

## 3. BAI JUYI (白居易, 772–846): THE POST-COLLAPSE CRITIC

Born after the rebellion, Bai Juyi represents the second generation: those who inherited the simplified state and documented its extractive character.

### 3.1 "The Song of Everlasting Sorrow" (长恨歌, 806)

**Context:** A long narrative poem telling the story of Emperor Xuanzong's flight from Chang'an, the mutiny that forced him to execute his consort Yang Guifei, and his subsequent grief. Written roughly 50 years after the events, it became the canonical literary treatment of the rebellion.

**Analysis:** The poem treats the collapse as tragedy rather than catastrophe. Xuanzong is not a villain but a flawed protagonist whose personal love is destroyed by his political failure. The framing makes the collapse legible as moral drama rather than incomprehensible disaster—which may itself be a signature feature of how second-generation collapse literature processes what the first generation could only witness.

**Thematic coding:**

| Variable | Coding | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Legitimacy narrative | **Ambivalent.** The emperor is presented sympathetically; his failure is registered but also humanized. | The poem does not attack the dynasty as illegitimate; it mourns the human cost of imperial weakness. |
| Elite self-indictment | **Present but gentled.** The emperor's weakness caused the collapse, but the weakness is treated as tragic rather than contemptible. | Compare the much harsher elite self-indictment in late Roman Christian literature. |
| Temporal mood | **Elegiac, aestheticized.** The poem is not a call to action; it is a beautiful object made from catastrophe. | The aestheticization itself may be a signal: when collapse becomes material for art rather than argument, a political turning point has been passed. |

**Confidence: Level A.** Text attribution and date secure.

### 3.2 New Yuefu Social-Critique Poems (新樂府, c. 806–810)

**Context:** A series of roughly 50 poems modeled on Han dynasty folk-song forms, each addressing a specific social problem under the post-collapse fiscal system.

**Key poem: "The Old Charcoal Seller" (賣炭翁)**

> 賣炭翁，伐薪燒炭南山中。
> 滿面塵灰煙火色，兩鬢蒼蒼十指黑。
> ...
> 一車炭，千餘斤，宮使驅將惜不得。
> 半匹紅紗一丈綾，繫向牛頭充炭直。

**Translation (key lines):**
> Old charcoal seller, cutting wood and burning charcoal in the southern hills,
> Face covered with dust and ash, temples grey, ten fingers black.
> ...
> A cartload of charcoal, over a thousand catties—
> Palace agents drive it away, and he dares not protest.
> Half a bolt of red gauze, a yard of silk,
> Tied to the ox's head—that's the payment.

**Thematic coding:**

| Variable | Coding | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Legitimacy narrative | **Broken at street level.** The state extracts value (the charcoal) without providing anything resembling fair exchange. The "palace agents" are the visible face of an extractive apparatus that has lost its protective function. | This is the Confucian imperial bargain documented as broken at the level of ordinary economic transaction. |
| Corruption/decadence language | **Present, systemic.** The critique is not of individual corruption but of a system that routinely extracts value from the powerless. | This is more precise than generic "decadence" language. It's a structural critique in poetic form. |

**Confidence: Level A.** Text attribution and date secure.

## 4. HAN YU (韓愈, 768–824): THE FOUNDATIONS REVIVAL

### 4.1 "Memorial on the Bone of Buddha" (諫迎佛骨表, 819)

**Context:** A formal memorial to Emperor Xianzong protesting the emperor's plan to publicly venerate a Buddhist relic (a supposed finger bone of the Buddha). The memorial got Han Yu exiled to the far south, where he nearly died.

**Key passage:**
> 佛本夷狄之人，與中國言語不通，衣服殊制。口不言先王之法言，身不服先王之法服。... 況其身死已久，枯朽之骨，凶穢之餘，豈宜令入宮禁？

**Translation:**
> The Buddha was a man of the barbarians. His language was not Chinese; his clothes were of a different cut. He did not speak the model words of the Former Kings; he did not wear the model robes of the Former Kings. ... Moreover, he has been dead for a long time. His decayed bone, his ill-omened and filthy remnant—how can it be right to let it enter the imperial palace?

**Thematic coding:**

| Variable | Coding | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Renewal scripts | **"Return to foundations" explicitly argued.** Han Yu is attempting to reassert Confucianism against Buddhism as the civilization's operating system. The argument is that the Tang has lost its way by adopting foreign practices and must return to its roots. | This is the "return to foundations" pattern identified in the method memo as a potential recurrent signal. |
| Legitimacy narrative | **Contested.** Han Yu is arguing that the current court's legitimacy is compromised by its embrace of non-Chinese culture. He is attempting to *reconstruct* legitimacy on Confucian foundations. | Compare Augustine's attempt to reconstruct Christian legitimacy after the sack of Rome. |
| Moral-order fracture | **Diagnosed and prescription offered.** The fracture is named (Buddhism has displaced Confucianism) and a remedy is proposed (return to the Former Kings' model). | This is the most explicit diagnosis-and-prescription in the corpus. |

**Confidence: Level A.** Text attribution and date secure.

## 5. CROSS-CORPUS PATTERNS (PRELIMINARY CODING)

### 5.1 Recurrent Motifs Across the Tang Corpus

| Motif | Authors | Cross-case resonance |
|---|---|---|
| The state is broken but nature endures | Du Fu ("Spring Prospect") | Compare: Ugarit letters (RS 88.2009), where the city is described as abandoned but the land remains |
| The golden age as organizing memory | Du Fu, Bai Juyi | Compare: Augustine (Rome as lost norm), late Habsburg literature (Roth, *Radetzky March*) |
| The state extracts but no longer protects | Bai Juyi, Du Fu | Compare: Salvian (*On the Government of God*), late Romanov literature |
| Return to foundations | Han Yu | Compare: Augustine (*City of God*), Slavophiles, aspects of contemporary New Right |
| Personal displacement as cultural loss | Du Fu, Li Bai, Wang Wei | Compare: Stefan Zweig (*The World of Yesterday*), late Roman correspondences |
| Aestheticization of collapse | Bai Juyi ("Song of Everlasting Sorrow") | Compare: Roth (*Radetzky March*), late Romanov literature—collapse becomes tragic beauty |

### 5.2 Patterns Absent from the Tang Corpus

| Absent motif | Present in | Possible significance |
|---|---|---|
| Apocalyptic/providential frame | Late Rome (Augustine, Salvian), late Romanov (Dostoevsky's *Demons*) | The Tang collapse was understood historically, not cosmically |
| Active elite self-indictment | Late Rome (Salvian), late Habsburg (Kraus) | Tang elite critique was gentler; the dynasty survived, so direct indictment was more dangerous |
| Utopian renewal vision | Late Rome (Augustine's *City of God*), early Ming | Tang post-collapse literature offers elegy, not vision |
