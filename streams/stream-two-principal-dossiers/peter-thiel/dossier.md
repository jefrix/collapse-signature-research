**Strategic Posture**: Stagnation critic and contrarian. Argues that technological progress has slowed since the 1970s; advocates for "definite optimism," monopoly power in key sectors, and skepticism of globalization and elite institutions. Strong emphasis on defense tech, AI for national security, and individual agency over collective solutions.

## 1. Core Framework & Ideological Claims

Key works and themes:
- *Zero to One* (2014): "Definite optimism" vs indefinite; monopoly as good for innovation; "the future is not evenly distributed."
- Critiques of "mad science" (AI safety extremism) and underinvestment in hard tech/defense.
- Network state and exit-oriented thinking (influenced Balaji and others).
- Recent (2020s): Strong focus on US-China tech competition; Palantir as example of building real capability.

Public stance frames AI as dual-use capability that must be harnessed for Western strength, not slowed by excessive safetyism.

## 2. System Alignment & Methodology v3 Coding

- **Narrative Classification**: `rising-but-vulnerable` (Sees US/tech civilization as having lost momentum; current AI moment as potential inflection for renewed ascent if monopoly builders and defense focus prevail).
- **Compensatory Fragility**: Emphasis on concentrated power (monopolies, strong founders, defense tech) may create new chokepoints or single points of failure if key nodes (e.g., specific companies or individuals) are targeted or fail.
- **Return-to-Foundations**: Explicit call to return to high-agency, founder-driven, "definite" progress of earlier eras (pre-1970s moonshot culture). Critiques of post-1970s stagnation and bureaucratic capture.
